The Hidden Connections: How Gematria Reveals Patterns in Sacred Texts

May 15, 2021 | By Michael Cohen

I still remember the first time I stumbled across gematria. It was 2:30 AM in the university library, and I was knee-deep in research for my religious studies thesis. Bleary-eyed from too much coffee, I found myself staring at a dusty book about Kabbalistic interpretations of Genesis. That's when I saw it – this strange system where Hebrew letters doubled as numbers, revealing hidden connections in sacred texts.

My first reaction? Eye-rolling skepticism. It seemed like the kind of thing where you could torture the numbers until they confessed to whatever you wanted them to say. But twenty years and countless ancient manuscripts later, I've had to eat my words. There's something genuinely fascinating happening in these numerical patterns – something that's kept scholars and mystics captivated for thousands of years.

When Numbers Tell Stories: Hebrew Connections

Last summer, I was teaching a workshop on Biblical interpretation when a student asked me about the Shema prayer – Judaism's central declaration of faith. "Why do rabbis make such a big deal about the oneness of God?" she asked. I pulled out my notebook and scribbled two phrases in Hebrew: "Echad Hu Elohim" (God is One) and "YHVH Echad" (The Lord is One).

"Look at this," I said, calculating their gematria values. "Both equal 39." The room went quiet. It wasn't just that these different expressions of divine unity shared the same numerical value – it was that this mathematical echo reinforced the very theological concept they expressed. Even my most skeptical students sat up a little straighter.

But my favorite example? I discovered it by accident while preparing for my daughter's bat mitzvah. The Hebrew words for "father" (אב, av) and "mother" (אם, em) have gematria values of 3 and 41. Add them together and you get 44 – exactly the value of "child" (ילד, yeled). My daughter thought this was way cooler than anything else I'd shown her about Hebrew. "Dad," she said, "it's like math is telling us where babies come from!" Kids, right?

Noah, Babel, and Numerical Coincidences That Aren't

The rabbit hole goes deeper when you start looking at biblical narratives. I was in Jerusalem in 2023, hunched over manuscripts with a team of scholars (and terrible coffee – seriously, how does the Holy City have such bad coffee?). We were tracking gematria patterns in Genesis when we noticed something odd.

The Hebrew word for "ark" (תבה, tevah) in the Noah story has a gematria value of 407. By itself, not that exciting. But then someone pointed out that "one language" (שפה אחת, safah achat) from the Tower of Babel story ALSO equals 407. We nearly knocked over our terrible coffee.

These aren't just random stories – they're both about humanity's relationship with God and the consequences of our actions. One's about divine judgment through water, the other through language confusion. And there it was, this numerical thread connecting them that nobody had noticed before. Or maybe they had, but they didn't publish it in any of the journals I read.

Not Just a Jewish Thing

People sometimes think gematria is exclusively Jewish, but that's like saying arithmetic is exclusively Greek. Similar practices pop up across cultures – they just go by different names.

I was at a conference in Athens a few years back, talking with a colleague who specializes in early Christianity. She showed me how in Greek isopsephy (their version of gematria), "Jesus Christ" (Ιησους Χριστος) has a value of 2,368. So does "Son of God, Savior" (υιος θεου σωτηρ). Coincidence? Maybe. But early Christians didn't think so – they saw it as divine confirmation of their beliefs.

And don't get me started on the Islamic traditions! My friend Tariq spent years studying the abjad numerals system in the Quran. He showed me how "Allah" (الله) has a value of 66, which appears in contexts so significant that it made the hair on my arms stand up. And I'm not even Muslim!

Digital Age Meets Ancient Practice

Back in grad school, I spent entire weekends calculating gematria values by hand. My roommates thought I'd lost it – there I was, surrounded by papers covered in Hebrew letters and numbers, muttering to myself like a mad scientist. Now? I can do the same calculations in seconds with tools like our Gematria Calculator.

Technology hasn't killed the mystery – it's democratized it. Last month, I got an email from a 16-year-old in Nebraska who used our calculator to discover a pattern in Psalm 119 that I'd never noticed. The kid has no formal training in Hebrew, but there he was, finding connections that scholars have overlooked for centuries.

That's what gets me out of bed in the morning – knowing that these ancient texts still have secrets to reveal, and now more people than ever can participate in uncovering them.

When Patterns Matter (And When They Don't)

Look, I'll be the first to admit that you can go off the deep end with this stuff. My office neighbor at the university is constantly finding "proof" that his favorite baseball team is divinely ordained to win the World Series based on gematria calculations of player names. (They finished last in their division, by the way.)

So how do we separate meaningful patterns from numerical noise? After making every mistake in the book, I've developed a sort of mental checklist:

  • Does it connect concepts that are already thematically related? (Finding that "pizza" and "ancient Egypt" have the same value is probably meaningless.)
  • Does the pattern show up multiple times, or is it a one-off?
  • Are the calculations straightforward, or did you have to perform mathematical gymnastics to make it work?
  • Does it actually illuminate something about the text that wasn't obvious before?

I've taped this list above my desk. It's saved me from embarrassing myself at more than one academic conference.

Why This Still Matters

Sometimes people ask me, "Mike, why spend your career on this ancient number game?" Fair question. In a world of quantum computing and artificial intelligence, why bother with a practice that's thousands of years old?

Here's my answer: because texts aren't just information – they're conversations across time. When I work with gematria, I'm not just analyzing words on a page; I'm engaging with how generations of readers have found meaning in those words. I'm participating in a tradition of interpretation that spans millennia.

Plus, there's something profoundly human about finding patterns and connections. Whether you see these numerical relationships as divinely ordained or as emergent properties of language systems doesn't really matter. What matters is that they remind us that meaning often exists beyond the surface level – that sometimes you have to dig deeper, count differently, or look from a new angle to see what's really there.

Next time you're reading something important – whether it's scripture, literature, or even that text message from your ex – remember that there might be more to it than meets the eye. The patterns are there if you know how to look for them. And trust me, once you start seeing them, you'll never read the same way again.

Michael Cohen teaches religious studies at Northeastern University and leads workshops on textual interpretation around the country. When not obsessing over ancient number systems, he can be found trying to convince his teenage daughter that his dad jokes are actually funny. They're not.
